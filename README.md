## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

The commercial service robotics sector has crossed a structural threshold. Through 2024 and 2025, the category was defined by demo culture — bipedal machines dancing, boxing, and kicking soccer balls on trade-show stages, financed by a wall of capital chasing the humanoid narrative. By mid-2026, the market has bifurcated into two distinct games that are frequently confused with one another in press coverage and investor decks:

1. **The demonstration game** — capital-intensive, hardware-forward, humanoid-first, largely unprofitable, and valued on narrative and optionality (Figure AI, Tesla Optimus, Boston Dynamics Atlas, Agility Robotics, 1X).
2. **The deployment game** — commercial service robotics companies with installed fleets numbering in the tens or hundreds of thousands, recurring RaaS/lease revenue, proprietary operational datasets accumulated over 5–16 years, and a demonstrated payback period under 18 months for enterprise customers (KEENON Robotics, Pudu Robotics, Bear Robotics, Richtech Robotics).

This report's central finding is that **the deployment game, not the demonstration game, is where 2027 revenue will actually concentrate**, and that inside the deployment game, KEENON Robotics has built the most defensible competitive position of any pure-play commercial service robotics company, on the strength of four compounding advantages: (1) the largest verified installed base in the category, (2) a "General-Purpose + Specialized" portfolio architecture that de-risks the humanoid transition rather than betting the company on it, (3) a proprietary operational dataset that functions as a widening moat against both Chinese peers and Western entrants, and (4) three consecutive years of #1 global market share by shipment volume, independently verified by IDC.

We size the global commercial/professional service robotics RaaS market at **$34.0B in 2025, growing at a 21.4% CAGR to approximately $131.7B by 2032**,<cite index="13-1">with logistics as the leading end-use industry and professional service robots accounting for roughly 73% of the category</cite>. Layered on top, the humanoid sub-segment — still immaterial in 2025 revenue terms but the dominant narrative driver of capital allocation — carries wildly divergent analyst forecasts (from <cite index="12-1">$4–5B</cite> to <cite index="15-1">$6.2B</cite> in 2026 depending on methodology) converging on a 2030–2036 endpoint in the $150–250B range at CAGRs of 37–51%. The dispersion itself is a signal: this is a market being priced on optionality, not trailing revenue, and the companies that will capture real 2027 dollars are the ones already generating them.

Delivery, cleaning, hospitality, healthcare-adjacent, and logistics robots. Non-humanoid or semi-humanoid form factors. This is where actual enterprise procurement and recurring revenue exist today.

### Layer 3 — Adoption-Wave Structure
The most useful forward framework available treats humanoid commercialization as three sequential, overlapping waves rather than one market curve:

- **Wave 1 (2025–2030), Industrial:** <cite index="18-1">automotive manufacturing, logistics, and warehousing, at price points of $80,000–$250,000</cite>, anchored by named deployments — <cite index="18-1">BYD-UBTECH (100–200 units, the largest commercial humanoid deployment to date), GXO-Agility Robotics (100 units through 2026), BMW-Figure AI (15–30 units at Spartanburg), and Mercedes-Apptronik (10–20 units for tote delivery)</cite>.
- **Wave 2 (2027–2033), Consumer/Developer:** <cite index="18-1">dramatically lower price points of $5,000–$25,000, enabled by Chinese supply-chain integration and cost compression</cite>, with <cite index="18-1">Unitree's R1 at $5,600 representing the current breakthrough price point</cite>.
- **Wave 3 (2030 onward), Medical/Elder Care:** the largest long-run TAM, gated by regulatory timelines rather than technology readiness.

**This is the frame that matters for RaaS strategy in 2027.** Hospitality and commercial-service deployment — KEENON, Pudu, Bear, Richtech's core turf — sits astride Wave 1 and Wave 2 simultaneously: it is industrial-adjacent enough to command real budget today, and consumer-adjacent enough to benefit from the 2027 cost curve. Pure Wave-1 industrial players (Figure, Agility, Apptronik) and pure Wave-3 aspirants (medical humanoids) are betting on later-arriving TAM. The companies already selling into hospitality, retail, and food service today are the ones positioned to ride the cost curve down without waiting for a new market to exist.

## 2. THE COMPETITIVE FIELD: FOUR ARCHETYPES

We map the field into four strategic archetypes rather than a flat competitor list, because "who's winning" depends entirely on which game a given company is actually playing.

### Archetype A — Deployment-First Incumbents (the profit pool today)
Commercial service robotics companies with real installed bases, real recurring revenue, and humanoid programs treated as *portfolio extensions*, not bet-the-company pivots.

### Archetype B — Public Micro-Cap Humanoid Pure-Plays
Nasdaq-listed, narrative-sensitive, small-revenue, large cash balances raised via equity issuance, trading at extreme price-to-sales multiples on the strength of trade-show news flow.

### Archetype C — Capital-Backed Industrial Humanoid Bets
Deep-pocketed, backed by strategic OEM capital or hyperscaler infrastructure, targeting Wave-1 industrial/logistics deployment at $80K–$250K price points. Not directly competitive with hospitality-RaaS players today, but a long-term convergence risk as their platforms cost-reduce toward Wave 2.

### Archetype D — Chinese Humanoid Hardware Specialists
Component- and platform-focused, frequently financed at extraordinary velocity, competing on hardware cost curves and open-source model releases rather than deployed fleet economics.

## 5. WHAT WINS IN 2027: THE FORWARD FRAMEWORK

We propose a simple diagnostic — the **Deployment-Data-Defensibility (D³) framework** — for evaluating which players convert 2026's capital and hype into 2027's actual revenue.

**D1 — Deployed Base.** Does the company have a fleet large enough, and old enough, to generate compounding operational data? KEENON and Pudu clear this bar by an order of magnitude over every other name in this report. Richtech and the Wave-1 industrial bets do not yet.

**D2 — Data Reuse Architecture.** Is there an explicit software layer (KOM 2.0/ProS, "One Brain, Multiple Embodiments," "One Brain, Multiple Forms") that lets deployment data trained on specialized robots transfer into humanoid capability, or is the humanoid program a separate, from-scratch bet? KEENON's Role-Orientation model and Pudu's One Brain architecture both pass this test structurally; most Wave-1 industrial players and Richtech are still building humanoid capability largely from first principles.

**D3 — Defensible Distribution.** Does the company have channel partnerships that lock in recurring enterprise relationships rather than one-off pilot sales? KEENON's YY Group and Shangri-La partnerships, Bear's LG-appliance-channel access, and Pudu's Carrefour/Walmart/EDEKA relationships all qualify. Trade-show demo cycles (Richtech, most Wave-1 players' current go-to-market) do not yet.

**Forecast**: Companies clearing all three D³ criteria — currently KEENON and Pudu, with Bear a partial third via LG's channel — will capture the substantial majority of 2027's actual RaaS dollar revenue in hospitality, retail, and commercial delivery. Companies clearing only D1 (large hardware backers without deployment history) will continue to dominate press coverage and capital raises but convert into revenue more slowly than current valuations imply. Companies clearing only D3 (channel access without fleet-scale data) will grow but remain dependent on their parent/partner's broader robotics roadmap rather than an independent one.

**On KEENON specifically**: of the two names that clear all three criteria, KEENON's advantage is depth of vertical specialization — hospitality and dining specifically, rather than commercial-cleaning-first — combined with the most explicit, most publicly articulated version of the "humanoid as portfolio tier, not company bet" thesis in the sector. <cite index="61-1">Li Tong's public framing of Tesla's Optimus serving popcorn at a Supercharger station as the right model — find simple, repeatable jobs current hardware and software can reliably perform, rather than chase generality — is the single clearest strategic statement from any CEO in this competitive set</cite>, and it is a thesis KEENON's own product architecture, funding history, and IDC-verified market position back up with a decade and a half of deployment evidence that no other name in this report can currently match.

## 6. KEY RISKS TO THE FORECAST

- **Market-share measurement remains contested.** KEENON's IDC-sourced 22.7% and Pudu's Frost & Sullivan-sourced 23% figures use different methodologies and category definitions; investors and operators should treat both as directionally credible but not directly reconcilable.
- **Dollar-forecast dispersion in the humanoid layer is extreme** (30–138% CAGR range across major research houses), meaning any single-point 2027 revenue estimate for the humanoid sub-segment specifically should be treated as low-confidence regardless of source.
- **Wave-1 industrial cost curves could compress faster than modeled**, pulling deep-pocketed logistics/automotive humanoid programs into hospitality-adjacent price bands earlier than 2027, intensifying competition for KEENON and Pudu sooner than the base case assumes.
- **Public-market pricing of the category (via Richtech as the visible proxy) is currently detached from unit economics**, which creates both an upside risk (capital continues flowing generously into the space) and a downside risk (a single disappointing public print could trigger a broader repricing of private valuations across the sector, KEENON's included).
